# 📊 Question Pattern Recognition Guide
**GEB 331 2012-2022 Analysis | Learn the Repeating Patterns**

This guide shows QUESTION TYPES that repeat across years. If you study the patterns, you can predict what might be asked.

---

## PATTERN A: "Explain the Mechanism" (40% of all questions)

**What it is**: A pathway is described, you must draw/explain the ENTIRE flow from start to finish.

**Examples from past exams**:
- Q013 (GPCR cascade): "Explain the complete G-protein signaling pathway from epinephrine binding to glycogenolysis"
- Q024 (Ras-MAPK): "Draw the RTK to MAPK pathway, showing all intermediates"
- Q040 (TGF-β): "Explain the SMAD pathway from TGF-β binding to gene expression"
- Q051 (UPR): "Describe the three branches of the ER stress response"

**How to identify**: Question says "Explain," "Describe," "Draw pathway," "Trace the signal"

**How students typically fail**: 
- Skip intermediate steps (just "A→X" instead of "A→B→C→X")
- Don't label what each step does (kinase? inhibitor? second messenger?)
- Forget the END point (what gene/protein is affected?)

**How to study for this**:
1. Draw EACH pathway by hand 3 times without notes
2. Practice explaining to a friend (teach-back)
3. Always include: START (ligand) → MIDDLE (proteins) → END (response)

**Exam Strategy**: 
- 3-5 marks: Simplified version (main players)
- 7-10 marks: Detailed (all relevant steps)
- 14 marks: EVERYTHING including regulation + disease context

---

## PATTERN B: "Compare and Contrast" (15% of questions)

**What it is**: Asked to find similarities AND differences between two related pathways.

**Examples**:
- Q005: "Compare synaptic vs endocrine signaling" (speed, range, specificity)
- Q011: "Contrast GPCR (Gs) vs GPCR (Gq)" (different G-proteins, different outcomes)
- Q045: "TGF-β vs BMP-2 signaling" (SMAD differences, tissue effects)
- Q056: "Normoxia vs hypoxia response" (PHD activity, HIF-1α fate)

**How to identify**: "Compare," "Contrast," "Difference between," "Similarity shared by"

**How students typically fail**:
- Only list one side (e.g., just what's different, not similarities)
- Miss the functional consequence (Who cares about difference X? What does it MEAN?)
- Organize poorly (messy comparison, hard to follow)

**How to study**:
1. Make 2-column table: "Pathway 1" vs "Pathway 2"
2. For EACH row, ask "Why does this difference matter?"
3. Find 3+ similarities + 3+ differences for major pathways

**Exam Strategy**:
- Structure: "Similarities: X, Y, Z. Differences: A→consequence 1, B→consequence 2"
- High-value insight: "This difference allows [specific regulation]"

## PATTERN C: "Mutation Consequence" (25% of questions)

**What it is**: Single point mutation or gene loss described, you explain cascading effects.

**Examples**:
- Q024: "Ras-G12V mutation → cancer" (can't turn OFF)
- Q034: "APC gene loss → cancer" (loses both suppression + allows Wnt)
- Q035: "IκBα mutant that can't be phosphorylated" (NF-κB always on)
- Hypothetical: "What if SMAD4 dominant-negative?"

**How to identify**: "mutation," "loss of function," "gain of function," "what would happen if..."

**How students typically fail**:
- Say "broken protein" without specifics (not enough detail)
- Only mention one consequence (usually the most obvious)
- Miss the MECHANISM (just say "cancer" without "because...")
- Don't distinguish gain-of-function from loss-of-function effects

**How to study**:
1. For each major protein, know: What does it normally do? What if absent? What if mutated?
2. Ask "Why does this mutation specifically break this protein?"
3. Link to real cancer types (Ras-G12V = pancreatic, lung; APC loss = colorectal)

**Exam Strategy**:
- "Normal: X is ON" → "Mutant: X is LOCKED ON" (or always OFF)
- "Result: pathway can't respond to [normal brake]"
- "Consequence: uncontrolled [phenotype]"

## PATTERN D: "Clinical Connection" (10% of questions)

**What it is**: Describe a disease or drug therapy, connect to the signaling pathway.

**Examples**:
- "Why does β-blocker drug prevent cardiac hypertrophy?" (GPCR Gs pathway)
- "Why does MEK inhibitor work for Ras-G12V cancers?" (downstream blockade)
- "Why do patients with HIF-1α mutations have reactive erythrocytosis?" (too much EPO)
- "Why does TGF-β inhibitor help early pancreatic cancer but might not late stage?"

**How to identify**: "Clinical," "Disease," "Therapy," "Drug," "Patient," "Treatment"

**How students typically fail**:
- Know the pathway but CAN'T CONNECT to disease (don't see the link)
- Medical terminology unfamiliar (e.g., "erythrocytosis" = too many RBCs)
- Don't explain the MECHANISM of therapy (just "works" instead of "works because...")

**How to study**:
1. After learning each pathway, ask "What goes wrong in disease?"
2. Find 2-3 diseases per major pathway
3. Practice explaining to non-scientist neighbor (simplify!!)"

**Exam Strategy**:
- State the problem clearly: "In [disease], [protein] is [broken/overactive]"
- Link to outcome: "This causes [phenotype] because [mechanism]"
- If therapy mentioned: "Drug restores [balance] by [blocking/activating]"

## PATTERN E: "Regulatory Mechanism" (20% of questions)

**What it is**: Focus on HOW pathways are turned ON and OFF (not just the main pathway).

**Examples**:
- "Explain GRK-β-arrestin desensitization of GPCRs"
- "How does GAP regulate Ras-GTP hydrolysis?"
- "What role does SMAD7 play in TGF-β signaling?"
- "How does phosphodiesterase regulate cAMP levels?"
- "Why is the ubiquitin pathway critical for NF-κB regulation?"

**How to identify**: "Regulation," "Feedback," "Inhibition," "Control," "Modulation," "GAP," "Phosphatase"

**How students typically fail**:
- Don't mention negative regulation (only "pathway goes ON")
- Confused about WHEN the regulation acts (during ON vs during OFF)
- Miss the evolutionary purpose (why multiple layers?)

**How to study**:
1. For EACH pathway, identify: 3+ "OFF switches"
2. Ask: "Which acts fastest? Slowest?"
3. Why multiple: "First layer = immediate stop, second layer = cleanup"

**Exam Strategy**:
- "The pathway is OFF because [brake protein X]"
- "When signal removed, [OFF mechanism Y] takes over"
- "This multi-layer regulation provides [benefit Z]"

---

## PATTERN F: "Evolution/Conservation" (5% of questions, but high-level depth)

**What it is**: Explains why something is conserved from bacteria to humans, or how it evolved.

**Examples**:
- "Bacterial chemotaxis (CheA-CheY) vs mammalian MAPK. Why similar architecture?"
- "Why is G-protein cycle conserved in evolution?" (robust, efficient)
- "Why do humans have 3 MAPK types (ERK, JNK, p38) but yeast has 1?" (complexity)

**How to identify**: "Conserved," "Evolution," "Why this system," "Advantage of," "Why not..."

**How to study**: 
- Think DEEP: "What makes this design good?" 
- Examples: Two-component systems = reliable. Cascade = amplification.
- Know the TRADE-OFF: If you gain X, you lose Y

**Exam Strategy**: Shows philosophical thinking (like a scientist, not memorize)

## PATTERNS NOT TO FALL FOR (Anti-Patterns)

### ❌ TRAP 1: "Quick answer questions"
Some questions look simple but have LAYERS:
```
Q: "What is cAMP?"
Simple answer: "Second messenger in GPCR pathway"
EXAM ANSWER: "cAMP is small diffusible molecule (MWt=330) that 
             amplifies GPCR signal ~1000x via PKA activation,
             degraded by phosphodiesterase (~1-5 sec half-life)"
```

### ❌ TRAP 2: "Assume the common answer is right"
```
Q: "Ras-G12V blocks what protein?"
Common WRONG answer: "Blocks GAP protein"
CORRECT: "GAP can't recognize G12V, so neither protein is 'blocked'
          but GAP can't do its job on that mutant"
```

### ❌ TRAP 3: "Different question, same concept"
If asked about different pathways, the PRINCIPLE is same:
```
All of these are "locked activation":
- Ras-G12V stuck GTP → Raf permanently on
- Notch S3 cleavage → NICD permanently stable
- CaMKII autophosphorylation → stays active
Pattern: "Something prevents normal OFF → constitutive ON"
```

## PATTERN STUDY SUPERPOWER

**Exercise**: Read PAST exam questions (if available), classify each by PATTERN

```
Example: 2022 Paper Question List
Q1 (14m) → PATTERN: Mechanism explanation (full pathway)
Q2 (7m) → PATTERN: Compare/contrast (signaling types)
Q3 (7m) → PATTERN: Regulatory (feedback control)
Q4 (5m) → PATTERN: Quick mechanism (one step)
Q5 (14m) → PATTERN: Mutation consequence (cancer)
Q6 (7m) → PATTERN: Clinical connection (drug therapy)
Q7 (3m) → PATTERN: Definition (term meaning)
...etc
```

**Insight**: Exam has DISTRIBUTION of patterns:
- 40% mechanism → need 3-4 detailed pathway diagrams
- 25% mutation → need 5-6 cancer examples studied
- 15% compare → need 5-6 comparison tables
- 10% clinical → need linked disease knowledge
- And so on...

**THIS DISTRIBUTION REPEATS EVERY YEAR**

So if you study TYPES and have EXAMPLES for each, you're essentially prepping for any question!

## Your Pattern Study Checklist

- [ ] Mechanism pathways: Know the 5 main pathways by heart (GPCR, RTK, TGF-β, Wnt/Notch, UPR)
- [ ] Comparisons: Know 6+ comparison pairs (Gs vs Gq, Notch vs Wnt, early TGF-β vs late)
- [ ] Mutations: Know 8+ mutations/losses and their effects (Ras-G12V, APC loss, GAP loss, etc.)
- [ ] Clinical connections: Link each pathway to 2-3 diseases + 2-3 drugs
- [ ] Regulation: For each pathway, know 3+ OFF switches
- [ ] Teach yourself: Practice explaining each pattern without reading notes
